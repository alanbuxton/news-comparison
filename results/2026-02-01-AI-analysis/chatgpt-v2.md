## How each provider behaves in practice

### 1. **Syracuse — Highest signal, lowest noise**

**What it does well**

* Consistently surfaces **material events**:

  * regulatory actions
  * litigation
  * restructurings
  * plant closures / expansions
  * geopolitical exposure
* Articles are **overwhelmingly real, attributable, and correctly dated**
* Strong bias toward:

  * Reuters
  * Argus
  * industry trade press
* Minimal duplication of:

  * earnings announcements
  * press-release rewrites
  * “CEO said…” commentary

**Evidence from your data**

* For **ExxonMobil**, Syracuse highlighted:

  * Venezuela re-entry constraints
  * CCS operational rollout
  * Sakhalin exit deadlines
  * refinery shutdowns due to weather
* For **International Paper**, it clearly captured:

  * company split
  * closures and layoffs
  * asset divestments
* For **WestRock**, it surfaced:

  * cartel litigation risk (Italy)
  * plant closures
  * structural post-merger impacts

These are exactly the facts that change **meeting posture and negotiation tone**.

**Limitations**

* Lower volume
* Will miss “soft” developments (branding, awards, minor partnerships)

**Best for**

* Pre-meeting briefs
* Supplier / counterparty risk assessment
* Internal strategy discussions
* “What do I need to know walking into the room?”

---

### 2. **LinkUp — Broad, fast, but noisy**

**What it does well**

* Very good coverage of:

  * mainstream financial media
  * press releases
  * earnings cycles
* Articles are **real and correctly dated**
* Useful as a **completeness check**

**Evidence from your data**

* Heavy repetition of:

  * Q4 earnings announcements
  * investor calls
  * awards and recognitions
* For WestRock and ExxonMobil, many articles said essentially the same thing with different headlines

**Weaknesses**

* High duplication
* Significant “corporate hygiene” noise:

  * earnings beats
  * dividend declarations
  * awards
  * sponsorships

This content rarely changes how you conduct a meeting.

**Best for**

* Investor-style monitoring
* Confirming nothing major was missed
* Background reading, not briefing

---

### 3. **Perplexity — Interesting but least reliable for this use-case**

**What it does well**

* Occasionally surfaces:

  * niche regional sources
  * non-English media
  * early-stage developments
* Helpful for **idea generation**

**Key concerns (based on your file)**

* **Mixed provenance**:

  * some sources are thinly attributed
  * some items feel synthesized rather than reported
* **Relevance drift**:

  * articles loosely related to the company but not *about* the company
* **Context dilution**:

  * merges strategic signals with generic commentary

**Examples**

* Sigma Chemtrade and smaller entities showed:

  * tangential market commentary
  * indirect ecosystem news
* Higher risk of:

  * false positives
  * over-interpreting weak signals

**Best for**

* Exploratory scanning
* Supplementary context
* Never as a sole decision input
